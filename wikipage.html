<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Noble train of artillery</h1>			<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>noble train of artillery</b>, also known as the <b>Knox Expedition</b>, was an expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> Colonel <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> to transport heavy weaponry that had been <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Capture of Fort Ticonderoga">captured at Fort Ticonderoga</a> to the Continental Army camps outside Boston, Massachusetts during the winter of 1775–1776.
</p><p>Knox went to Ticonderoga in November 1775 and moved 60 tons<sup id="cite_ref-Ware18_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ware18-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> of cannons and other armaments over the course of three winter months by boat, horse, ox-drawn sledges, and manpower along poor-quality roads, across two semi-frozen rivers, and through the forests and swamps of the lightly inhabited <a href="/wiki/Berkshires" class="mw-redirect" title="Berkshires">Berkshires</a> to the Boston area,<sup id="cite_ref-Ware19_24_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ware19_24-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brooks38_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks38-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> covering approximately 300 miles. Historian Victor Brooks has called Knox's exploit "one of the most stupendous feats of logistics" of the entire <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VBrooks210_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VBrooks210-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The route which he followed is now known as the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox_Trail" title="Henry Knox Trail">Henry Knox Trail</a>, and the states of New York and Massachusetts have erected markers along the way.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Boston_campaign" title="Boston campaign">Boston campaign</a></div>
<div role="navigation" class="navbox" aria-labelledby="Boston_campaign_1774–1776" style="margin:0;float:right;clear:right;width:25.5em;margin-bottom:0.5em;margin-left:1em;;padding:3px"><table class="nowraplinks collapsible autocollapse navbox-inner" style="border-spacing:0;background:transparent;color:inherit"><tbody><tr><th scope="col" class="navbox-title" colspan="2" style="background-color:#C3D6EF;"><div class="plainlinks hlist navbar mini"><ul><li class="nv-view"><a href="/wiki/Template:Campaignbox_American_Revolutionary_War:_Boston" title="Template:Campaignbox American Revolutionary War: Boston"><abbr title="View this template" style=";background-color:#C3D6EF;;background:none transparent;border:none;-moz-box-shadow:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none;box-shadow:none; padding:0;">v</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-talk"><a href="/wiki/Template_talk:Campaignbox_American_Revolutionary_War:_Boston" title="Template talk:Campaignbox American Revolutionary War: Boston"><abbr title="Discuss this template" style=";background-color:#C3D6EF;;background:none transparent;border:none;-moz-box-shadow:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none;box-shadow:none; padding:0;">t</abbr></a></li><li class="nv-edit"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Template:Campaignbox_American_Revolutionary_War:_Boston&amp;action=edit"><abbr title="Edit this template" style=";background-color:#C3D6EF;;background:none transparent;border:none;-moz-box-shadow:none;-webkit-box-shadow:none;box-shadow:none; padding:0;">e</abbr></a></li></ul></div><div id="Boston_campaign_1774–1776" style="font-size:114%;margin:0 4em"><span style="line-height:1.6em"><a href="/wiki/Boston_campaign" title="Boston campaign"><span class="wrap">Boston campaign</span></a><br /> 1774–1776</span></div></th></tr><tr><td colspan="2" class="navbox-list navbox-odd hlist" style="width:100%;padding:0px"><div style="padding:0em 0.25em">

<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> erupted with the <a href="/wiki/Battles_of_Lexington_and_Concord" title="Battles of Lexington and Concord">Battles of Lexington and Concord</a> in April 1775. <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a> was a militia leader from Connecticut who had arrived with his unit in support of the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">Siege of Boston</a>; he proposed to the Massachusetts Committee of Safety that <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Fort Ticonderoga">Fort Ticonderoga</a> on <a href="/wiki/Lake_Champlain" title="Lake Champlain">Lake Champlain</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_New_York" title="Province of New York">Province of New York</a> be captured from its small British garrison.  One reason that he gave to justify the move was the presence of heavy weaponry at Ticonderoga.  On May 3, the committee gave Arnold a Massachusetts colonel's commission and authorized the operation.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The idea to capture Ticonderoga had also been raised to <a href="/wiki/Ethan_Allen" title="Ethan Allen">Ethan Allen</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Green_Mountain_Boys" title="Green Mountain Boys">Green Mountain Boys</a> in the disputed <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_Grants" title="New Hampshire Grants">New Hampshire Grants</a> territory in Vermont.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Allen and Arnold joined forces, and a force of 83 men <a href="/wiki/Capture_of_Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Capture of Fort Ticonderoga">captured the fort</a> without a fight on May 10.  The next day, a detachment captured the nearby <a href="/wiki/Fort_Crown_Point" title="Fort Crown Point">Fort Crown Point</a>, again without combat.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Arnold began to inventory the two forts for usable military equipment,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but he was hampered by a lack of resources and conflict over command of the forts, first with Allen and then with a Connecticut militia company sent to hold the fort in June. He eventually abandoned the idea of transporting the armaments to Boston and resigned his commission.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Expedition_planning">Expedition planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noble_train_of_artillery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expedition planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 1775, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> assumed command of the forces outside Boston,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and one of the significant problems which he identified in the nascent <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> was a lack of heavy weaponry, which made offensive operations virtually impossible. It is not known who proposed the operation to retrieve the Ticonderoga cannons, but historians tend to credit either <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> or <a href="/wiki/Benedict_Arnold" title="Benedict Arnold">Benedict Arnold</a> with giving Washington the idea; regardless, Washington chose Knox for the job.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryKnox.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/70/HenryKnox.jpg/170px-HenryKnox.jpg" width="170" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/70/HenryKnox.jpg/255px-HenryKnox.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/70/HenryKnox.jpg/340px-HenryKnox.jpg 2x" data-file-width="716" data-file-height="935" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryKnox.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox" title="Henry Knox">Henry Knox</a> later in life</div></div></div>
<p>Knox was a 25 year-old bookseller with an interest in military matters who served in the Massachusetts militia, and he had become good friends with Washington on his arrival at Boston.  When Washington gave him the assignment, he wrote that "no trouble or expense must be spared to obtain them."<sup id="cite_ref-W19_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W19-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  On November 16, Washington issued orders to Knox to retrieve the cannons and authorized £1000 for the purpose, and he wrote to General <a href="/wiki/Philip_Schuyler" title="Philip Schuyler">Philip Schuyler</a> asking him to assist Knox in the endeavor.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks38_9_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks38_9-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Washington's call for the weapons was echoed by the <a href="/wiki/Second_Continental_Congress" title="Second Continental Congress">Second Continental Congress</a>, and they issued Knox a colonel's commission in November—although it did not reach him until he returned from the expedition.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks34_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks34-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Knox departed Washington's camp on November 17 and traveled to New York City for supplies, reaching Ticonderoga on December 5. He shared a cabin with a young British prisoner named <a href="/wiki/John_Andr%C3%A9" title="John André">John André</a> at <a href="/wiki/Fort_George,_New_York" title="Fort George, New York">Fort George</a> at the southern end of Lake George.  André had been taken prisoner during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Fort_St._Jean" title="Siege of Fort St. Jean">Siege of Fort St. Jean</a> and was on his way south to a prison camp.  The two were of a similar age and temperament, and found much common ground to talk about.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The next time they met, however, Knox presided over the court martial which convicted and sentenced André to death for his role in Benedict Arnold's treason.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noble_train_of_artillery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryKnoxTrail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/HenryKnoxTrail.jpg/240px-HenryKnoxTrail.jpg" width="240" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/HenryKnoxTrail.jpg/360px-HenryKnoxTrail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/HenryKnoxTrail.jpg/480px-HenryKnoxTrail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2453" data-file-height="1892" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HenryKnoxTrail.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The apparent route of the expedition, overlaid on a 1779 map</div></div></div>
<p>Knox's letters and diaries provide the primary sources for much of the daily activity in this journey. His description is detailed for some of the events and dates, but there are also significant gaps, and significant portions of the journey are poorly documented, especially much of the Massachusetts section.  Some of these gaps occur because Knox did not write about them, and others because pages are missing from the diary.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Other sources confirm some of Knox's details or report additional details, but parts of the route are not known with certainty, and modern descriptions of those parts are based on what is known about Massachusetts roads at the time, including the placement of markers for the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox_Trail" title="Henry Knox Trail">Henry Knox Trail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, the route was more or less in the corridor of today's Massachusetts Turnpike (Interstate 90).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Albany">Albany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noble_train_of_artillery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Albany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Upon arrival at Ticonderoga, Knox immediately set about identifying the equipment to take and organizing its transport.<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks40_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks40-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  He selected 59 pieces of equipment, including cannons ranging in size from four to <a href="/wiki/24-pounder_long_gun" title="24-pounder long gun">twenty-four pounders</a>, mortars, and howitzers.  He estimated the total weight to be transported at 119,000 pounds (about 60 tons or 54 metric tons).  The largest pieces, the twenty-four pounders, were 11 feet (3.4&#160;m) long and estimated to weigh over 5,000 pounds (2,300&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-W19_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W19-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The equipment was first carried overland from Ticonderoga to the northern end of <a href="/wiki/Lake_George_(New_York)" title="Lake George (New York)">Lake George</a>, where most of the train was loaded onto a <a href="/wiki/Scow" title="Scow">scow</a>-like ship called a <a href="/wiki/Gundalow" title="Gundalow">gundalow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-W19_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W19-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  On December 6, the gundalow set sail for the southern end of the lake, with Knox sailing ahead in a small boat.  Ice was already beginning to cover the lake, but the gundalow, after grounding once on a submerged rock, reached Sabbath Day Point.  The next day, they sailed on, again with Knox going ahead.  While he reached Fort George in good time, the gundalow did not appear when expected.  A boat sent to check on its progress reported that the gundalow had foundered and sunk not far from Sabbath Day Point.  While this at first appeared to be a serious setback, Knox's brother William, captain of the gundalow, reported that she had foundered, but that her <a href="/wiki/Gunnel_(ship_element)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gunnel (ship element)">gunnels</a> were above the water line, and that she could be bailed out.  This was done, the ship was refloated, and two days later, the gundalow arrived at the southern end of the lake.<sup id="cite_ref-W20_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W20-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 17, Knox wrote to Washington that he had built "42 exceeding strong sleds, and have provided 80 yoke of oxen to drag them as far as <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_Massachusetts" title="Springfield, Massachusetts">Springfield</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-W21_2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W21_2-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and that he hoped "in 16 or 17 days to be able to present your Excellency a noble train of artillery".<sup id="cite_ref-Brooks40_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brooks40-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Schuyler.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Schuyler.jpg/220px-Schuyler.jpg" width="220" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Schuyler.jpg/330px-Schuyler.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Schuyler.jpg/440px-Schuyler.jpg 2x" data-file-width="504" data-file-height="536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Schuyler.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>General <a href="/wiki/Philip_Schuyler" title="Philip Schuyler">Philip Schuyler</a></div></div></div>
<p>Knox then set out for Albany ahead of the train. At <a href="/wiki/Glens_Falls,_New_York" title="Glens Falls, New York">Glens Falls</a>, he crossed the frozen <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a> and proceeded on through <a href="/wiki/Saratoga,_New_York" title="Saratoga, New York">Saratoga</a>, reaching New City (present-day <a href="/wiki/Lansingburg,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Lansingburg, New York">Lansingburg</a>), just north of Albany, on <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>.  Two feet (0.6 m) of snow fell that day, slowing his progress, as the snow-covered route needed to be broken open.  The next day, again slowed by significant snow on the ground, he finally reached Albany.  There, he met with General <a href="/wiki/Philip_Schuyler" title="Philip Schuyler">Philip Schuyler</a>, and the two of them worked over the next few days to locate and send north equipment and personnel to assist in moving the train south from Lake George.  While the snowfall was sufficient for the use of sleds to move the train overland, the river ice was still too thin to move it over the Hudson.  Knox and his men tried to accelerate the process of thickening the river ice by pouring additional water on top of existing ice.  By January 4, the first of the cannon had arrived at Albany.  On the way to Albany, and again on crossing the Hudson heading east from there toward <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, cannons crashed through the ice into the river.  In every instance, the cannon was recovered.  On January 9, the last of cannons had crossed the Hudson, and Knox rode ahead to oversee the next stage of the journey.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossing_the_Berkshires">Crossing the Berkshires</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noble_train_of_artillery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Crossing the Berkshires">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg/220px-Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg" width="220" height="271" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg/330px-Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg/440px-Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg 2x" data-file-width="509" data-file-height="627" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henry_Knox_entering_camp_with_artillery_cph.3g09060.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>19th-century drawing depicting the arrival of the weapons.</div></div></div>
<p>Details of the remaining journey are sketchy, as Knox's journal ends on January 12.  He reached the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Claverack,_New_York" title="Claverack, New York">Claverack, New York</a>, on January 9, and proceeded through the <a href="/wiki/Berkshires" class="mw-redirect" title="Berkshires">Berkshires</a>, reaching <a href="/wiki/Blandford,_Massachusetts" title="Blandford, Massachusetts">Blandford, Massachusetts</a>, two days later.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  There, the lead crew refused to continue owing to a lack of snow and the upcoming steep descent to the <a href="/wiki/Connecticut_River" title="Connecticut River">Connecticut River</a> valley.  Knox hired additional oxen and persuaded the crew to go on.  As the train moved further east, news of it spread, and people came out to watch it pass.  In <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Massachusetts" title="Westfield, Massachusetts">Westfield</a>, Knox loaded one of the big guns with powder and fired it, to the applause of the assembled crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-W24_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W24-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At Springfield, Knox had to hire new work crews, as his New York-based crews wanted to return home.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/John_Adams" title="John Adams">John Adams</a> reported seeing the artillery train pass through <a href="/wiki/Framingham,_Massachusetts" title="Framingham, Massachusetts">Framingham</a> on January 25.  Two days later, Knox arrived in Cambridge and personally reported to Washington that the artillery train had arrived.  According to Knox's accounting, he spent £521 on an operation he had hoped would take two weeks, that instead took ten weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival">Arrival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noble_train_of_artillery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Arrival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Fortification_of_Dorchester_Heights" title="Fortification of Dorchester Heights">Fortification of Dorchester Heights</a></div>
<p>When the equipment began to arrive in the Boston area, Washington, seeking to end the siege, formulated a plan to draw at least some of the British out of Boston, at which point he would launch an attack on the city across the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a>.  Pursuing this plan, he placed cannons from Ticonderoga at <a href="/wiki/Lechmere_Square" title="Lechmere Square">Lechmere's Point</a> and Cobble Hill in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>, and on Lamb's Dam in Roxbury.<sup id="cite_ref-VBrooks224_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VBrooks224-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  These batteries opened fire on Boston on the night of March 2, while preparations were made to fortify the <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_Heights" title="Dorchester Heights">Dorchester Heights</a>, from which cannons could threaten both the city and the British fleet in the harbor.  On the night of March 4, Continental Army troops occupied this high ground.<sup id="cite_ref-French406_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-French406-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VBrooks225_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VBrooks225-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SiegeBoston.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A black and white engraving, with horsemen on the left and workmen on the right. Behind them are the waters of a harbor with sailing ships at anchor." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/SiegeBoston.jpg/220px-SiegeBoston.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/eb/SiegeBoston.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="323" data-file-height="215" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SiegeBoston.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An engraving depicting the evacuation of British troops from Boston</div></div></div>
<p>British General <a href="/wiki/William_Howe,_5th_Viscount_Howe" title="William Howe, 5th Viscount Howe">William Howe</a> first planned to contest this move by assaulting the position, but a snowstorm prevented its execution.  After further consideration, he decided instead to withdraw from the city.  On March 17, British troops and <a href="/wiki/Loyalist_(American_Revolution)" title="Loyalist (American Revolution)">Loyalist</a> colonists boarded ships and sailed for <a href="/wiki/City_of_Halifax" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Halifax">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VBrooks228_30_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VBrooks228_30-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Henry Knox went on to become the chief artillery officer of the Continental Army, and later served as the first <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">United States Secretary of War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Noble_train_of_artillery&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Henry_Knox_Trail" title="Henry Knox Trail">Henry Knox Trail</a></div>
<p>To commemorate Knox's achievement, at the time of its sesquicentennial (150th anniversary), the states of <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> both placed historical markers along the route he was believed to have taken at the time.  In 1972, markers in New York were moved when new information surfaced about the train's movements between <a href="/wiki/Albany,_New_York" title="Albany, New York">Albany</a> and the state boundary.  Most of the markers in Massachusetts are along a route the train was assumed to take, given the sparsity of documentation and what was known about roads in Massachusetts at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fort_Knox" title="Fort Knox">Fort Knox</a>, an Army post in <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> most famous for being the site of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bullion_Depository" title="United States Bullion Depository">United States Bullion Depository</a>, was named after Henry Knox.
</p>
